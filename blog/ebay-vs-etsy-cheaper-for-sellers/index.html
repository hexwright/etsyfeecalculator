eBay vs Etsy — Which is Cheaper for Sellers in 2026?

Both platforms charge sellers differently. eBay uses a final value fee model with no listing cost for most sellers. Etsy charges per listing plus a transaction fee. But when you add up every cost on an identical sale, the answer might surprise you — and it changes depending on what you sell.

Understanding Each Platform's Fee Structure

eBay and Etsy both take a cut of every sale — but they get there in completely different ways. eBay built its fee model around auctions and resale: minimal upfront costs, with a percentage taken on the final sale value. Etsy, on the other hand, was designed for creators selling unique or handmade goods, and it charges a small flat fee just to list an item, then a transaction fee on top.

The result is a fee structure that can favor different sellers in different situations. A seller moving high volumes of inexpensive items will have a different experience than a maker selling one-of-a-kind pieces at a premium price. Before you can compare the two platforms fairly, you need to understand what each one actually charges — and when those charges apply.

ℹ️

Important: Both platforms update their fee schedules periodically. All figures in this guide reflect 2026 rates as of June 2026. Always verify current rates on the official eBay and Etsy fee pages before making business decisions.

The key fees to understand on each platform are: listing fees, transaction/final value fees, payment processing fees, and any additional optional or mandatory fees like promoted listings, subscriptions, or off-site advertising. We'll walk through each platform individually before comparing them head to head.

eBay Seller Fees Breakdown 2026

eBay has simplified its seller fee model significantly over the past few years since introducing Managed Payments. Here's what sellers pay in 2026:

eBay Insertion (Listing) Fee

Most eBay sellers get 250 free listings per month. After that, each listing costs $0.35 (US). This applies to both fixed-price and auction listings. If you're a casual or medium-volume seller, you'll likely never pay an insertion fee at all. Sellers with a Basic or above eBay Store subscription get even more free listings — up to 10,000 depending on the plan.

eBay Final Value Fee (FVF)

This is eBay's main revenue mechanism. The Final Value Fee is a percentage of the total sale amount — that includes the item price and shipping charges. In 2026, the FVF rates are:

On top of the percentage, eBay also charges a $0.30 per order fee (separate from the insertion fee). This flat per-transaction charge applies to every completed sale.

eBay Managed Payments Processing

Since eBay moved all sellers to Managed Payments, payment processing is now bundled into the final value fee structure rather than charged separately. There is no separate PayPal fee for most sellers — eBay handles the entire payment flow and pays out to your bank account.

However, if a buyer pays with a payment method that has a higher interchange cost (such as certain credit cards), eBay absorbs that difference — it doesn't pass it on to you as a variable rate. This makes eBay's effective payment cost more predictable than Etsy's.

eBay Store Subscription (Optional)

eBay Store plans are optional but can reduce fees for high-volume sellers:

  • Starter Store: $4.95/month — 250 free fixed-price listings
  • Basic Store: $21.95/month — 1,000 free fixed-price listings, reduced FVF on select categories
  • Premium Store: $59.95/month — 10,000 free listings, further FVF discounts
  • Anchor Store: $299.95/month — 25,000 listings, lowest FVF rates available

eBay Promoted Listings (Optional)

eBay's Promoted Listings Standard allows you to boost visibility in search results and pay only when a buyer clicks your ad and purchases within 30 days. The ad rate is variable — typically 5%–15% of the sale price — set either by you or eBay's suggested rate. This is entirely optional but increasingly important for visibility in competitive categories.

Etsy Seller Fees Breakdown 2026

Etsy's fee model has several components that stack up on every sale. Unlike eBay, Etsy charges a fee just to put an item in your shop — before anything sells. Here's the complete breakdown:

Etsy Listing Fee

Every item listed on Etsy costs $0.20 per listing. This fee is charged when you publish a listing, and again every time a listing auto-renews (every 4 months if unsold). When an item sells, if you have multiple quantities in stock, a new listing is automatically created for each additional sale — so you'll pay $0.20 again per multi-quantity item sold.

For sellers with large inventories or frequently refreshed stock, listing fees can add up meaningfully. A shop with 500 active listings pays $100 just to keep those items live.

Etsy Transaction Fee

Etsy charges a 6.5% transaction fee on the total order amount — this includes the item price, shipping charges, and any gift wrapping fees you charge. This changed from the old 5% rate in 2022, and it has remained at 6.5% through 2026. The fee applies to the full amount the buyer pays you, not just the item price.

Etsy Payment Processing Fee

If you use Etsy Payments (which is mandatory in most countries including the US and UK), you pay a payment processing fee on every transaction. In the United States this is 3% + $0.25 per transaction. UK sellers pay 4% + £0.20. This is in addition to the transaction fee — it's a separate charge.

Etsy Offsite Ads (Mandatory for High-Volume Sellers)

Etsy automatically advertises your products on Google, Facebook, Instagram, Pinterest, and Bing. If a buyer clicks one of these ads and purchases from your shop within 30 days, Etsy charges an advertising fee:

  • Sellers under $10,000 annual revenue: 15% offsite ads fee, and you can opt out
  • Sellers over $10,000 annual revenue: 12% offsite ads fee, and you cannot opt out

This fee applies on top of all other fees when triggered. For high-earning Etsy sellers, it's essentially a mandatory cost of doing business on the platform.

Etsy Plus Subscription (Optional)

Etsy Plus costs $10/month and includes 15 listing credits ($3 value), $5 in Etsy Ad credits, and access to advanced shop customization tools like custom web addresses and restock request alerts. For most sellers, the credits don't offset the full $10 cost.

Which is Cheaper by Product Category?

The fee comparison isn't the same for all product types. Here's how it plays out across different seller categories:

Handmade Items (Jewelry, Art, Ceramics, Candles)

For handmade goods at prices above $20, Etsy is almost always cheaper in raw fees. But more importantly, Etsy's built-in audience is specifically searching for handmade products. Higher conversion rates on Etsy mean lower effective customer acquisition cost — which matters more than a 3–4% fee difference.

Vintage Clothing and Collectibles

Both platforms support vintage. eBay has historically had a larger audience for vintage collectibles, trading cards, and nostalgia items. For vintage clothing specifically, Etsy and eBay now compete more directly. At price points under $30, eBay's per-order fee and higher FVF make Etsy slightly more attractive. At higher prices ($80+), Etsy clearly wins on fees.

Used Electronics and Tech

eBay dominates this category. Etsy does not allow the sale of most used electronics (unless they are vintage — defined as 20+ years old). For used phones, tablets, laptops, and gaming gear, eBay is the only real option between the two platforms.

Craft Supplies

Etsy allows craft supplies and they tend to sell well there to its built-in maker audience. eBay also handles craft supplies, but with a broader, less-targeted audience. Fee-wise, Etsy wins for higher-priced supply bundles. For low-cost supplies under $10, the $0.20 listing fee per item starts to eat significantly into margins.

Print-on-Demand Products (T-shirts, Mugs, Prints)

Etsy allows print-on-demand integrations with Printify and Printful. eBay allows POD too. Etsy's audience is significantly better for this type of product. Fee-wise, Etsy is cheaper per sale. If you're running a POD business, Etsy is the clear choice between these two platforms.

Hidden Costs Sellers Often Miss

The fees listed above are only part of the story. Both platforms have additional costs that can significantly impact your actual profit margin — and many sellers don't account for them until they're already losing money.

Etsy Offsite Ads — The Big One

If your Etsy shop makes more than $10,000 in a 12-month period, you are permanently enrolled in Etsy's Offsite Ads program and cannot opt out. Every time a buyer arrives from an Offsite Ad and purchases within 30 days, you pay an additional 12% of the total order value. On a $75 sale, that's an extra $9 — on top of the $8.34 in regular fees. Your effective fee rate on that sale jumps from 10% to 22%+.

Sellers under the $10,000 threshold pay 15% but can opt out. Most new sellers leave it on, thinking it will drive traffic. It can — but at a steep cost per sale.

eBay Promoted Listings — Increasingly Necessary

eBay's organic search visibility has declined for many sellers as the platform pushes Promoted Listings. While technically optional, many sellers find that without promoting their listings, sales volumes drop significantly. The promoted rate varies by category and season, but budgeting 8–12% on top of the FVF is realistic for most categories. This brings eBay's effective total fee to 21–25% for many promoted sellers — comparable to or higher than Etsy's offsite ads rate.

Etsy Listing Renewal Fees at Scale

Running a shop with 300 active listings means paying $60 every 4 months just to keep them live — $180 per year before a single item sells. If your items sell slowly, the listing fee cost per unit rises materially. Sellers with large, slow-moving catalogues will feel this more than eBay sellers with equivalent inventories.

Returns and Refunds

Both platforms require sellers to handle refunds. eBay's Money Back Guarantee policy is buyer-friendly and can sometimes favor buyers in disputes. Etsy's purchase protection program similarly protects buyers. Neither platform refunds the listing fee or transaction fee for returned items that were already dispatched, though Etsy does refund the transaction fee for cancelled (not-shipped) orders.

⚠️

Don't forget shipping label costs. Both platforms offer discounted shipping labels through their built-in tools. eBay's shipping label discounts can be significant — sometimes up to 30–40% off USPS/UPS rates. Etsy also offers shipping discounts through Etsy Shipping. Factor this in when choosing: the platform that saves you more on labels could offset a higher transaction fee.

UK sellers face a slightly different fee structure on both platforms. Here's a quick comparison for a £30 sale (£25 item + £5 shipping) in the UK market:

Listing fee £0 (first 1,000/mo) £0.16 per listing
Final value / Transaction fee 12.8% + £0.30 per order 6.5% + 4% + £0.20
Effective combined rate on £30 ~£4.14 ~£3.36 + £0.16 listing
Winner at £30 ✅ Etsy (by ~£0.62)

UK eBay sellers benefit from slightly lower FVF rates than US sellers in most categories (12.8% vs 13.25%). UK Etsy sellers pay a higher payment processing fee (4% vs 3% in the US) but overall Etsy still works out cheaper for most UK sales above £20. The breakeven point where the two platforms cost roughly the same is approximately £15–£18 per order for UK sellers.

Final Verdict — Which Should You Choose?

📊 Our Verdict

For most sellers, Etsy charges less in total fees per sale — but eBay has advantages that matter more than fees for some categories.

If you sell handmade, vintage, or creative products at prices above $20–$25, Etsy's combined rate of roughly 9.5%–10% beats eBay's 13.25%+ in most scenarios. The Etsy $0.20 listing fee is real but easily offset by the fee gap at higher price points.

If you sell used goods, electronics, mass-market resale, or anything that doesn't fit Etsy's handmade/vintage/craft supply niche, eBay is your only viable option between the two — and despite higher fees, its massive buyer base typically delivers higher sales volume.

The most profitable approach for eligible sellers is often both platforms simultaneously — Etsy for handmade and unique items, eBay for inventory clearance, used goods, or anything with broader mass-market appeal.

When to Choose eBay

  • You sell used, refurbished, or mass-produced items
  • Your products don't qualify for Etsy (used electronics, new mass-market goods)
  • You want auction-style listings for rare or collectible items
  • You sell internationally — eBay's global marketplace is larger
  • You have a high-volume business and want a Store subscription to reduce FVF

When to Choose Etsy

  • You sell handmade, custom, or made-to-order products
  • You sell vintage items (20+ years old)
  • You sell craft supplies to other makers
  • Your items are priced above $25 and margins matter
  • You want a shopfront that reflects your brand identity as a maker
  • You run a print-on-demand business
